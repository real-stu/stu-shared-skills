# @girls Story Series Skill

This skill produces 10-slide branded Instagram Story Series for 10PM Curfew's @girls account — the interactive poll format that consistently clears 97K+ engagements per partnership and has hit 246K on its best runs. It covers the full workflow from brand intake → game selection → slide-by-slide copy → CTA close.

---

## What Is an @girls Story Series?

A single-session run of 10 Instagram Stories, every slide branded "Presented by @[brand]," that uses an interactive binary poll on each slide to drive engagement. The audience taps through the whole series because each slide is a fast, playful choice — not an ad. The brand earns attention by entertaining first and selling only on the final slide.

**The structure that works:**
1. **Slide 1 — Intro/hook** — "LET'S PLAY" + game name + edition subtitle + brand tag
2. **Slides 2-9 — 7-8 poll slides** — one binary poll per slide with a creative headline
3. **Slide 10 — CTA close** — direct offer, tap-to-action sticker, brand logo

**Performance benchmarks across 6 deployed series:**

| Partner | Engagements | Published Slides |
|---|---|---|
| Sev Laser (3/18/25) | 246,000 | 7 |
| Sev Laser (5/16/25) | 232,000 | 10 |
| Picsart (4/8/26) | 148,000 | 10 |
| Face Foundrie (1/4/26) | 117,000 | 10 |
| European Wax Center (4/16/26) | 97,000 | 10 |

View retention across the 10 slides typically holds 55-65% from slide 1 to slide 10, with 97-99% of views coming from existing followers.

## Humor & Virality: The Non-Negotiable Principle

**The series lives or dies on whether it's funny.** Every slide should feel like a text your funniest friend would send at 11pm — not a slide from a brand deck. The only reason this format clears 100K+ engagements is that followers tap through because it's entertaining, not because it's branded. Strip the logo off any proven slide and it would still land in a group chat.

If the output reads polished, polite, or professional, it has already failed. Default to humor, specificity, and a hint of unhinged. Never too serious.

**Three humor mechanics that drive the results:**

### 1. Relatable-Unhinged Specificity (the Picsart Move)
The jokes that hit hardest name a very specific behavior that feels too real. Specificity is the single strongest lever you have — always go more specific, more weirdly particular, more "wait, did they write this about me?"

- ✅ "They send you 17 edited versions of the same photo"
- ❌ "They overshare edits"
- ✅ "They replaced your ex with a houseplant"
- ❌ "They edit out exes"
- ✅ "They edited themselves into your baby photo"
- ❌ "They photoshop themselves into your pics"

The number "17," the houseplant, the baby photo — those concrete details are what make people screenshot it.

### 2. The Screenshot-to-Group-Chat Test
Before any poll slide ships, ask: **would a follower see this and immediately screenshot it to send to their group chat with "this is literally you" or "WHY IS THIS SO ACCURATE"?** If no, the slide is filler. Virality in this format is literally measured in followers forwarding the series to other people — a poll that doesn't earn a forward is a poll that's too safe.

Every 200K+ engagement series has at least 2-3 slides that pass this test hard.

### 3. The Aunt Test
If you can imagine someone's aunt writing a headline in a corporate email or LinkedIn post, rewrite it. "Look your best this summer" fails the Aunt Test. "Panic 2 hours before the pool party" passes. Anything generic, aspirational, inspirational, or HR-friendly is a dead slide.

**Rewrite triggers** — if you catch yourself writing any of these, stop and start over:
- "Feel confident," "Be your best self," "Embrace your..."
- Anything that could appear in a wellness brand's Pinterest board
- Balanced, neutral phrasing where neither option has a point of view
- Polite. Polite is the kiss of death in this format.

### What "Entertaining" Actually Looks Like
- Absurdist escalation (the joke keeps getting more specific and more unhinged)
- Named-and-shamed behaviors the audience recognizes guiltily in themselves
- Comedic pairings where BOTH options feel uncomfortably true
- Cultural observations so specific they feel surveilled
- A slight edge of chaos — this is not a brand's content, it's @girls content

## Game Format Selection

Choose the game format based on what the brand does. Five proven variants:

### This or That
**Default choice.** Works for most categories. Binary A/B between two lifestyle options.
- Best for: beauty, aesthetics, skincare, fashion, food, any category with natural "comparison" framing
- Used for: Sev Laser, Face Foundrie

### Red Flag or Green Flag
**Use when the product solves a relatable pain point** the audience recognizes as bad behavior.
- Best for: tech, creative tools, apps, dating-adjacent products, anything where misuse/overuse is a cultural joke
- Used for: Picsart (over-editing photos)
- Mechanic: present behaviors and let the audience judge them

### Hot Takes (Agree/Disagree)
**Use when the product is service/location-based** and the audience has strong opinions about the lifestyle around it.
- Best for: waxing, nails, fitness, wellness services, travel, anything with a strong "the girls know" energy
- Used for: European Wax Center (vacation prep)
- Mechanic: first-person declarative statements that are relatable or slightly unhinged

### Today's Craving: Plate A or B
**Use when options can be shown as photos** — especially food or visually driven products.
- Best for: food/CPG, fashion, jewelry, anything where the photo IS the message
- Used for: Atkins
- Mechanic: two product photos, minimal text, audience picks

### Custom-themed variant
**Use when the brand has a signature phrase or concept** that can carry the series.
- Rare, but high-upside when it lands
- Requires the brand's own language (e.g. a tagline, slogan, or internal shorthand)

## The 10-Slide Structure

### Slide 1 — Hook

Required elements:
- "LET'S PLAY" prefix (small caps, pill/button treatment)
- Game name customized to brand
- Edition subtitle (seasonal, product-specific, or cultural moment)
- "Presented by @[brand_handle]"
- Tap-forward cue (arrow, "Pick Your Preference," etc.)
- Brand color palette

**Edition subtitle formulas that have worked:**
- `[Product category] Edition` → "Glow-Up Edition," "Editing Pics Edition 2.0"
- `[Season/moment] Edition` → "Bikini Weather Edition," "Vacation Prep Edition"
- `[Year] Edition` → "2026 Edition" (Face Foundrie)
- `[Theme] Edition` → "Dinner Plate A or B — Atkins Edition"

### Slides 2-9 — The Polls

Every poll slide needs:
- **Creative headline** (2-5 words, top of slide) — playful, not literal
- **Two options** only — text pairs, photos, or concepts
- **"Presented by @[brand]"** visible on every single slide
- **Brand color palette** held consistently

**Headline formulas (all must pass the Aunt Test):**
- Aspirational noun phrase with a weird twist: "Cheekbones on cloud nine," "Pout Perfection"
- Cultural observation that's almost TOO specific: "They edited you into a Vogue cover for your birthday"
- First-person declaration, slightly unhinged (for Hot Takes): "I don't start packing until the night before"
- Emotional pairing where both sting a little: "Sweet Nicknames" vs "Inside Jokes"
- Punchy, slightly weird, a little funny. If a headline sounds like a magazine article, it's dead on arrival.

**Option-writing principles:**
- Phrase options like a group text at 11pm, not a catalog entry
- Specificity > vagueness, every time. "17 edited versions" beats "a lot of edits." A number or a weirdly concrete object beats a general description.
- When pitting brand against non-brand, make the non-brand option feel like a comedy of errors ("Panic 2 hours before the pool party")
- When both options are products, make both sound desirable so the audience feels the agony of choice
- When both options are behaviors, make both feel guilty-relatable so the audience can't pick without exposing themselves
- For food: use photos, minimal text labels
- If you can imagine your aunt writing it, rewrite it

### The Soft-Sell Slot

**One poll per series** (and only one) can position the brand's product as the clearly superior choice against a weak alternative. This is the sales slot.

Proven examples:
- Face Foundrie: "FACE FOUNDRIE Facial" vs "Anything else"
- Sev Laser: "Book that SEV appt" vs "Panic 2 hours before the pool party"

More than one soft-sell slot breaks the format — the series starts reading like an ad and engagement collapses. Pick one, protect it.

### Slide 10 — CTA Close

Required elements:
- One-line payoff tying back to the game ("Ready before takeoff." / "Eat what hits. Feel good after." / "Glow-up with a FREE consultation!")
- The offer (free trial, discount, consultation, download, booking)
- Tappable sticker — "TAP TO FIND A LOCATION NEAR YOU," "TAP TO TRY," "TAP TO DOWNLOAD"
- Hashtag when natural (#AtkinsApproved)
- Brand logo/mark

## Non-Negotiable Rules

1. **Binary only.** Two poll options per slide. Never three. The format breaks otherwise.
2. **"Presented by @[brand]" on every single slide.** Not just the hook and close.
3. **One cohesive brand color palette across all 10 slides.** This is what makes the series read as one unit in the story tray.
4. **Creative headlines, not product descriptions.** "Snatched & Smooth," not "Under-eye treatment comparison."
5. **One soft-sell slot max.** More than one and it tips into ad territory.
6. **The series must entertain even if you strip the brand.** Same test as fact posts — value-first, brand-earned.
7. **It has to be funny.** Not "mildly amusing." Actually funny — at least 3 slides should make the writer smirk or laugh while drafting. The series fails the Laugh Audit if it doesn't.
8. **The Aunt Test is law.** If any slide sounds like an aunt's corporate email, a LinkedIn post, or a wellness brand's Pinterest caption, rewrite it. No polished, polite, or HR-friendly language survives.
9. **Specificity over generality, always.** Numbers, weirdly concrete objects, named behaviors. "17 edited versions" beats "a lot of edits." "Houseplant" beats "something random."
10. **Humor comes from the category, not the catalog.** The joke is about the *archetype, scene, or cultural behavior* the brand lives inside — not about the brand's products. A skincare sponsor: jokes about beauty-girl behavior, not about serums. A supplement sponsor: jokes about wellness-girl culture, not about magnesium. A dating app sponsor: jokes about dating behavior, not about swipes. The brand is the reason the series exists; it is not the subject of the jokes. Product references belong on slide 10 and, if earned, one soft-sell slot. Never on the punchline slides.
11. **The poll slides must still be funny with the brand removed.** This is the acid test. If you strip "Presented by @[brand]" and the logo from a poll slide and the joke no longer works, the joke was never the joke — the brand was. Rewrite.

## Full Workflow

### Step 1: Brand Intake

When given a new brand, extract:
- What do they sell? (products, services, category)
- What cultural moment / season / life stage are we hitting?
- What's the target action? (book appointment, download app, try product, visit location)
- What's their brand color palette and tone? (Pull from brand skill if one exists, or the brand's live site.)
- What's the brand handle for "Presented by @[brand]" tagging?

### Step 2: Select Three Distinct Game Formats

**Always produce three options, not one.** The pitch model is: give the brand a real menu of three fully-developed series so they can pick a direction rather than approve or reject a single idea.

Pick three formats that differ *meaningfully* — not three flavors of the same thing. The differentiation axis is **tone and audience posture**, not just format name. A strong three-option set covers:

- **One "safer / observational" option** — audience judges someone else's behavior (e.g. Red Flag or Green Flag). Lowest audience self-exposure, highest forwardability.
- **One "self-reflective / sharp" option** — audience mirrors their own behavior (e.g. This or That). Strongest conversion setup because the CTA answers a problem the audience has already admitted to.
- **One "punchy / polarizing" option** — audience owns an opinion (e.g. Hot Takes). Highest virality ceiling.

Default trio when all three fit: **This or That / Red Flag or Green Flag / Hot Takes**. For brands where one of those doesn't map, swap in an alternative from the Game Format Selection section (Today's Craving, or a custom-themed variant) — but always keep the three-way tonal spread.

### Step 3: Edition Subtitle (per option)

Draft 2-3 edition subtitle candidates *per option*, then pick the strongest one for each. Each edition subtitle should:
- Tie to a current cultural moment or seasonal context when possible
- Feel specific to this brand's cultural lane, not generic
- Work inside the chosen format (e.g. "Hot Takes" pairs naturally with an "Edit" or "Take" subtitle; "Red Flag Green Flag" pairs with a "[Relationship/Role] Edition")
- Be said in 4 words or fewer

### Step 4: Poll Slide Generation — Write All Three Series

For each of the three options, write a full 10-slide series (hook + 8 polls + CTA close). Each option must stand on its own — not be a diluted or hypothetical version.

For each poll slide:
- 2-5 word creative headline
- Two options with playful phrasing
- Brief note on visual direction (photos vs text, brand colors)

Distribute the content so:
- Slides 2-3 open with wide, relatable hooks to pull the audience in
- Middle slides (4-7) can go more specific/branded
- One slot per option is the designated soft-sell
- Slide 9 should set up the CTA emotionally

**Across the three options, vary the brand-earning approach:**
- At minimum, two slides per option should quietly set up Enhanced's (or the brand's) positioning through category observation — not product demo
- Slide 7 in each option is the designated soft-sell slot
- Slide 10 is where the brand fully earns its moment

### Step 5: CTA Write (per option)

For each option, draft the closing slide with:
- One-line payoff that ties back to that option's game mechanic ("Whichever one you are..." for This or That, "If your bestie is going to be this intense..." for Red Flag Green Flag, etc.)
- Placeholder offer (confirm with brand: free consult, discount, free first treatment, download)
- Tap-to-action sticker language
- Optional hashtag

### Step 6: The Laugh Audit (HARD GATE — must pass before delivery)

This is a blocking step applied **to each of the three series independently**. Every option has to clear all four checks. If one fails, rewrite until it passes. No "we'll polish it later," no "the design will make it funnier," no exceptions.

**Check 1: The Smirk Count.** Re-read all 7-8 poll slides in this option. How many made you actually smirk, grin, or laugh while writing them?
- 3+ slides → Pass.
- Fewer than 3 → **Fail.** Identify the dullest slides and rewrite them. Push for more specificity, weirder options, more unhinged phrasing.

**Check 2: The Screenshot-to-Group-Chat Test.** For each poll slide, ask: would a follower screenshot this and send it to their friends with "THIS IS LITERALLY YOU" or "why is this so accurate"?
- At least 2 slides that clearly pass → Pass.
- Can't confidently point to 2 → **Fail.** The series is playing it too safe.

**Check 3: The Aunt Test.** Read every headline and option out loud. Does any of it sound like something a corporate aunt would put in a LinkedIn post, brand email, or Pinterest wellness caption?
- Nothing sounds aunt-coded → Pass.
- Anything sounds polished, polite, aspirational, or HR-friendly → **Fail.**

**Check 4: The Category-Not-Catalog Test.** For each poll slide, ask: is the joke about the *category* the brand lives in — or is the joke about the brand's *products*?
- Every poll slide is category-based → Pass.
- Any poll slide is catalog-based → **Fail.** Rewrite.

### Step 7: Internal Brand-Safety Audit (NOT shared with client)

**This is an internal check only. The outputs of this step never appear in client-facing files. Never include the words "risk," "brand risk," "flag," "caveat," or "warning" in any PowerPoint slide, Word paragraph, or speaker note that goes to the client.**

While drafting the three series, privately audit each poll line for potential brand-safety concerns specific to the partner:

- **Regulated categories** (prescription brands, supplements, health, financial, alcohol, tobacco): Watch for lines that could attract regulatory attention, make implicit medical claims, or disparage competing products from the same category the brand sells into.
- **Sensitive subject matter**: Watch for lines that touch on medical gaslighting, mental health crisis language, disordered eating, or anything that could read as mocking a clinical condition.
- **Competitive shade**: If the brand sells X, a poll that mocks X (even a competitor's version) is an internal own-goal.
- **Cultural third-rails**: Politics, religion, identity-based humor.

**How to handle what you find:**
- **Quietly rewrite** any flagged line before it makes it into the client files. A joke can almost always be relocated to a safer target while preserving the humor.
- **Do not present the client with a "risky" option and label it as such.** If a concept as a whole has too much exposure to be safely produced, replace it with a different format from Step 2 before file generation.
- **Do not include "strategic notes," "risk flags," or cautionary framing in client-facing deliverables.** The pitch is always three confidently-delivered options.

The only time risk gets named in a client-facing way is if Stu specifically asks for it outside of a client deliverable — for example, if he asks in conversation "what are the risks with this one?" then honest answer is appropriate. But the PowerPoint and Word files must read as a pure pitch, not a pitch-with-caveats.

### Step 8: Quality Check (per option)

Before generating deliverables, verify each option:
- [ ] Every slide has "Presented by @[brand]"
- [ ] Exactly one soft-sell slot per option, no more
- [ ] Headlines are creative, not descriptive
- [ ] Options read like a group text, not a catalog
- [ ] The CTA ties back to the game conceptually
- [ ] Color palette note is included for the design team
- [ ] The series entertains even if you remove the brand mentions
- [ ] The Laugh Audit passed all four checks
- [ ] At least 2 slides contain relatable-unhinged specificity
- [ ] No slide reads like corporate, aspirational, or wellness-coded language
- [ ] Every poll slide still works as a joke if you strip the brand logo off it
- [ ] Internal brand-safety audit completed; any flagged lines rewritten (Step 7)

### Step 9: Generate Both Deliverables

**Always produce both a PowerPoint pitch deck AND a Word document, every time. Do not ask the client which format they prefer — produce both.**

#### PowerPoint Pitch Deck (5 slides total for a 3-option pitch)

The PowerPoint is a **lean storyboard deck**, not a detailed walkthrough. Every option lives on a single storyboard slide that lays out all 10 IG stories at once.

**Deck structure:**
1. **Cover** — Brand name, "Story Series Pitch," date, 10PM Curfew mark.
2. **Comparison page** — Three-column table comparing the three options on tone, virality ceiling, conversion strength, audience posture, and best-for use case. Recommendation row at bottom.
3. **Option 01 — storyboard slide** (single slide, see layout spec below)
4. **Option 02 — storyboard slide** (same layout)
5. **Option 03 — storyboard slide** (same layout)

That's the entire deck. No per-IG-slide detail pages. No section dividers. No CTA offer pages. No benchmarks page. The Word doc carries all the longer-form content.

**Storyboard slide layout (this is the spec for slides 3, 4, 5):**

The slide is a 16:9 widescreen page with a **white / clean neutral background** (never pink, never the brand color — the white lets the IG mockups read cleanly).

Layout, top to bottom:

- **Header title** (large, dark navy or near-black, centered): `GIRLS - [Brand Name] STORY SERIES [N] - [Edition Name] Option [N]`
  - Example: `GIRLS - Physician's Choice STORY SERIES 1 - May Option 1`
  - Use the brand's actual capitalization (e.g. "Physician's Choice," not "PHYSICIAN'S CHOICE")
- **Legend** (small, top-left corner, three rows):
  - Gray swatch — "Link sticker feature"
  - Light yellow swatch — "Poll sticker feature"
  - Blue swatch — "Image"
- **10-up grid** of mini IG-story mockups, arranged 2 rows × 5 columns:
  - Each mockup is a portrait-oriented card (taller than wide), light gray fill with a thin border
  - Card 1 (top-left) = Slide 1 hook · Card 10 (bottom-right) = Slide 10 CTA close
  - Reading order is left-to-right, top row first, then bottom row

**What each mini-mockup contains:**

*Hook card (Slide 1):*
- Top center: "LET'S PLAY" (small caps)
- Middle: Game name in large bold black text (e.g. "WHO'S TO BLAME?")
- A right-pointing arrow shape below the title
- Bottom: "Presented By @[brand]"

*Poll cards (Slides 2-9):*
- Top center: 2-5 word headline in bold (e.g. "Bloating," "Brain fog")
- Below headline: Option A text + small emoji
- Two stacked **yellow horizontal bars** in the middle (these represent the IG poll stickers — the legend's yellow swatch ties to these)
- Below the yellow bars: Option B text + small emoji
- Bottom: "Presented by @[brand]"

*CTA close card (Slide 10):*
- 2-3 short lines of body copy that land the punchline (e.g. "Not saying it's your gut… but it might be your gut 👀")
- Optional middle line transitioning to the offer (e.g. "So I switched to something I'll actually take…")
- Final line: the product/CTA reference (e.g. "Probiotic and Fiber Gummies @ Walmart 🛒")
- Bottom: "Presented by @[brand]"

**Visual conventions inside the mockups:**
- Yellow horizontal bars = poll sticker (use across slides 2-9)
- Gray rectangle = link sticker (use only when the slide actually carries a link sticker, typically the CTA close)
- Blue rectangle = placeholder for an image (use on slides where the option requires a product photo or visual)

**Design tokens:**
- Slide background: white (`#FFFFFF`) or near-white off-white. Never pink. Never the brand color.
- Mini-mockup cards: light neutral gray fill (e.g. `#E8E8E8`) with thin darker gray border
- Poll sticker bars inside mockups: light yellow (`#FFE9A8` or similar)
- Title text and mockup body text: dark navy / near-black for legibility
- The brand's actual palette is used **inside** the mockups only where natural (e.g. brand emoji, brand handle treatment) — the deck itself is intentionally neutral so the IG content stands out.

#### Word Document (unchanged ~8 pages for a 3-option pitch)

The Word doc is where the detailed pitch lives — full slide-by-slide scripts, designer notes, strategic context.

- Cover + "How to read this document"
- Option 01 — full script (hook, 8 polls, CTA close, strategic note)
- Option 02 — same structure
- Option 03 — same structure
- Side-by-side comparison table
- Recommendation + next step

**For the Word doc design tokens:**
- Match the brand's actual palette (pull hex values from the brand's own skill if one exists, or from their live site).
- Use the brand's primary CTA color for accent moments — section headers, callout rules, benchmark stat numbers.
- Use a bone / off-white tone (e.g. `#B8B0A8`) for small repeating labels.

#### File construction & QA

**When generating deliverables, follow pptx and docx skills for file construction.** After writing, QA by converting to PDF/JPG and spot-checking that:
- Storyboard mockups don't overflow (10 cards fit cleanly in the 2×5 grid)
- Text inside mockups is legible at the small scale — push for short copy if anything looks tight
- Yellow poll bars are clearly differentiated from gray link bars
- Title bar reads the right brand and edition

**Present both files to the user in a single response** using the present_files tool with both file paths. Do not make the user ask for the second one.

## Final Notes

- **Always ask about the offer before finalizing Slide 10.** Can the brand support a free consult, discount code, free first treatment, free download? The CTA has to be something real.
- **Always produce three distinct series options and both deliverables (PowerPoint + Word) in a single response.** Do not produce a single option. Do not produce only a PowerPoint or only a Word document. The pitch model is always "three fully-developed series, two file formats, one confident presentation."
- **Client-facing files never surface brand risk.** The internal brand-safety audit in Step 7 shapes what gets written, but its output never appears as "risk flags," "caveats," or cautionary language in the PowerPoint or Word files. Three equally-polished options go out every time.
- **When in doubt, go shorter on headlines.** Three words beats five. "Snatched & Smooth" beats "Looking Snatched and Feeling Smooth All Summer."
- **When in doubt, go weirder.** If you're torn between a safe option and an unhinged option, the unhinged one wins. The safe one is why your series clears 40K instead of 240K.
- **Default to humor, always.** The moment a slide starts sounding like brand marketing, rewrite it. @girls followers tap through because they're entertained, not because they're being sold to.
- **The Sev Laser results (246K on 7 slides, 232K on 10 slides) suggest a 10-slide format is the current default but quality over quantity still wins — don't pad to 10 if the polls aren't strong.** A 7-slide series that passes the Laugh Audit will always outperform a 10-slide series that doesn't.
