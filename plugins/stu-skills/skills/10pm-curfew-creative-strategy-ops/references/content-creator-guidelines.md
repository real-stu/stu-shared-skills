# **Content Creator Guidelines**

**Purpose:** the goal is simple. Turn in content that's good enough to go live on the first round. Every revision costs you and us time, and most revisions come down to things that are completely avoidable with a little prep. Follow this and you'll get there faster, get booked more, and build a reputation as someone who's easy to work with.

---

**I. Before you film, prepare like a pro**

* Read the full brief and guidelines twice before you touch your camera. Know the one core message and the one call-to-action by heart.   
* Identify the single most important thing this video needs to do. If you can't say it in one sentence, re-read until you can.   
* Confirm the technical specs up front: orientation, length, format, and where it's posting.  
* If anything is unclear, ask your point of contact before you film, not after. One question now saves a full reshoot later. Asking is professional, not a weakness.

**II. Study the inspo video. This is the most important step.** 

* The inspo we send you has already been approved through multiple people and channels. It is the target, not a loose suggestion. Your version should land close to it.  
* Watch it three times, each time for a different reason:   
  * First watch: get the overall feel and message.   
  * Second watch: break down the structure beat by beat. Where's the hook? How long before they get to the point? When does the CTA land.  
  * Third watch: note the details. Pacing, energy, tone, framing, how they're sitting or standing, the setting, the editing rhythm.   
* Match what made it work: the hook, the order of information, the length, the energy level, and the shot type. These are the elements that got it approved.   
* Replicating is not copying. You are hitting the same beats and structure, but in your own words, your own face, your own delivery. Do not impersonate the original creator. Do not script yourself word-for-word off theirs.   
* The sweet spot: a stranger should be able to watch the inspo and your version back to back and recognize the same idea, told by two different real people.

**III. Make it feel authentic** 

* Talk like you're telling a friend something you're actually excited or worried about. Do not read like an ad.   
* Authentic does not mean off-script. Know your beats cold, then deliver them naturally so it never sounds memorized.   
* Use your own phrasing. The words that feel natural coming out of your mouth will always outperform someone else's words you're forcing.   
* Energy carries through the screen. Slightly more energy than feels normal usually reads as just right on camera.

**IV. Pay close attention to the collaboration do's and don'ts** 

* Every brand has specific do's and don'ts. These exist because of legal, compliance, and platform rules, and they are not optional or up for interpretation.   
* Read them carefully before you film and keep them in view while you work. Most rejected videos break a rule the creator simply skimmed past.   
* Use the approved language exactly as written. If a phrase is required, say it the way it's given. If a phrase is off-limits, do not work around it or improvise something close.   
* Watch for the off-camera rules too: products that can't appear, claims you can't make, words you can't say, accounts or competitors you can't show.   
* If a do or don't seems to conflict with the inspo or the brief, stop and ask your point of contact before filming. Do not guess.

**V. Make it feel authentic to the brand and to you** 

* Talk like you're telling a friend something you're actually excited or worried about. Do not read like an ad.   
* Authentic does not mean off-script. Know your beats cold, then deliver them naturally so it never sounds memorized.   
* Use your own phrasing within the approved language. The words that feel natural coming out of your mouth will always outperform someone else's words you're forcing.  
* Energy carries through the screen. Slightly more energy than feels normal usually reads as just right on camera.

**VI. Production quality, the non-negotiables** 

* Vertical, single take, eye-level, framed from roughly the chest up.   
* Good light on your face. Face a window or a light source. Never film with bright light behind you.   
* Clean audio. Quiet room, no echo, no background noise. Bad audio alone gets a video sent back.   
* Steady shot, clean background, nothing distracting behind you unless the brief calls for it.

**VII. Self-check before you submit. Do not skip this.** 

* Rewatch your video right next to the inspo. Be honest: does yours hit the same beats with the same energy?   
* Run the basics: Is the hook in the first three seconds? Is the core message clear? Is the CTA correct and easy to act on?   
* Quality pass: lighting clean, audio clear, no awkward pauses, nothing distracting.   
* The scroll test: if this came across your feed, would you stop and watch? If not, fix it before sending.

**VIII. The mindset that gets you booked again** 

* Treat every brief like the job it is. First-round quality is what separates creators who get rehired from creators who don't.   
* When in doubt, over-communicate with your point of contact. Clarifying up front is always faster than revising after.   
* Your goal every single time: turn in something you'd be proud to put your name on, that we can take live with zero changes.
