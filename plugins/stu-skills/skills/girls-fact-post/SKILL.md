# @girls Fact-Style Instagram Post Skill

This skill produces branded fact-style Instagram post captions for 10PM Curfew's @girls account. This is the format that has driven individual posts to 21M+ views. It covers the full workflow from brand intake to headline ideation to full caption writing with scientific sourcing and brand integration, for any partner category.

---

## What Is a Fact-Style Post?

A fact-style post leads with a bold, provocative, or surprising science-backed headline rendered as graphic text on the image and mirrored in the caption. The science does all the persuasion work. The brand appears at the end as the logical solution to the problem the science just identified. Never as an ad.

**The post should be worth reading even if the brand mention is removed entirely.**

---

## Post Structure

1. **Graphic headline** - Bold, all-caps, single provocative stat or claim (also the image text)
2. **Caption hook** - 1-2 sentences that expand or reframe the headline
3. **Supporting facts** - 2-4 bullet-style stats or science points that validate the claim. Use a plain hyphen "-" as the bullet character, not "•" or other glyphs.
4. **Pivot line** - One sentence bridging the science to the human/emotional experience
5. **Brand integration** - Transition into partner mention; feels earned, not inserted
6. **Closing line** - Emotional punch, reframe, or call to action
7. **Scientific source** - Cited naturally inline, not academically
8. **Hashtags** - 2-3 thematic tags

## Brand Integration Models

Choose based on the partner type:

### Model A: Science → Product Solution (Functional Bridge)
**Best for:** Health tech, supplements, biotech, telehealth, wearables, functional food brands
- Build a complete standalone scientific argument first
- Brand enters as the entity that built a solution around the science the reader just learned
- Transition phrase: **"That's why @[brand] created..."** or **"That's why @[brand] built..."**
- Reader feels they discovered the product through curiosity, not an ad

**Examples:** Hapbee (binaural beats → EMF neckband), Enhanced (hormone science → clinician protocol), Atkins (weight loss mirroring → low-carb snacks)

### Model B: Cause → Brand Values (Emotional Bridge)
**Best for:** Mission-driven brands, awareness campaigns, lifestyle brands with a purpose angle
- Lead with cause, awareness, or emotionally validating content
- Brand appears as the entity that *cares enough* to partner on the issue
- Founder voice or practical playbook deepens the connection
- Product is secondary. Brand equity comes from cause association

**Examples:** SheFit (breast cancer awareness → bra brand), period health brands, mental health adjacent brands

### Model C: Lifestyle Fact → Product Moment (Relatability Bridge)
**Best for:** Food, beverage, beauty, relationship/lifestyle brands
- Lead with a surprising or entertaining lifestyle/behavioral fact
- Brand appears as a natural part of the lifestyle being described
- Lighter tone. Witty and warm rather than science-heavy
- Works well with cultural moments, relationship dynamics, everyday habits

**Examples:** Atkins (GLP-1 couples sharing meals → low-carb snacks), food brands tied to social behaviors

## Tone & Voice Guide

- Conversational, Gen Z-adjacent but not forced
- Stats delivered casually. "Science backs it up," not academic
- No emojis anywhere in the caption (including paragraph breaks). Use line breaks and white space for rhythm instead
- No em dashes (—). Use periods, commas, colons, parentheses, or line breaks. If a list item or bullet is needed, use a plain hyphen "-"
- Empowering and validating. Never fear-based or shame-driven
- Witty without being sarcastic
- The brand pivot must feel *earned*, not inserted
- Short sentences. White space. Punchlines land on their own line.

## Headline Formula

Great @girls fact-style headlines share these traits:
- All caps
- Contain a specific, surprising, or counterintuitive stat or claim
- Often deliver a second gut-punch revelation using a period, comma, or the word "AND" (do not use em dashes)
- Validate something the audience already feels but hasn't been told is real
- Create a "wait, what?" moment that demands a read

**Proven structural templates:**
- `[SURPRISING FACT]. AND [IMPLICATION MOST PEOPLE DON'T KNOW]`
- `DOCTORS SAY [THING WOMEN EXPERIENCE] IS ACTUALLY [SCIENTIFIC EXPLANATION]`
- `[PRODUCT/THING] DOESN'T JUST [OBVIOUS BENEFIT]. IT [HIDDEN BENEFIT]`
- `BY AGE [X], YOUR BODY HAS LOST [SPECIFIC THING]`
- `SCIENTISTS IDENTIFIED THE [THING] THAT CONTROLS [OUTCOME], AND [SURPRISING DETAIL]`
- `[PERCENTAGE]% OF WOMEN [EXPERIENCE THING]. AND MOST DOCTORS NEVER CHECK FOR IT`

## Brand Intake Process

When approaching a new brand partner, extract or research:

1. **What do they sell?** Products, protocols, services, mechanisms
2. **What's the science behind it?** What does it actually do biologically or behaviorally?
3. **Which @girls content pillar does it map to?** (see table above)
4. **What audience segment?** All women, specific age group, specific condition
5. **Which integration model fits?** A (functional), B (cause), or C (lifestyle)
6. **What's the brand's existing tone?** Match or complement, never clash
7. **Are there regulatory/claims constraints?** (health brands especially. Use "may support," "has been associated with," etc.)

## Scientific Sourcing Standards

Every fact-style post should include at least one real, citable source. Place it after the closing line and before the hashtags, in line with the new structure (position 7 of 8).

**Format:**
```
Source: [Author(s)] et al., "[Study Title]," [Journal], [Year]
```
Or for institutional/survey sources:
```
Source: [Organization], "[Report/Survey Name]," [Year]
```

Do not prefix the source with an emoji (no 📚 or similar). Plain text only.

**Key verified sources by topic (confirm before publishing):**
- Female testosterone decline: Davis SR et al., Lancet Diabetes Endocrinol, 2015
- Hormone/burnout: Soares CN, Psychiatr Clin North Am, 2017

- GHK-Cu peptide decline: Pickart L & Margolina A, Int J Mol Sci, 2018

## Full Workflow

### Step 1: Brand Intake
Gather or research the brand using the intake process above. If a website or brand document is provided, read it fully before proceeding.

### Step 2: Headline Ideation
Generate 6-10 bold headline options grouped by topic angle. Prioritize:
- Headlines that validate dismissed female experiences
- Stats with a specific number (age, percentage, multiplier, timeframe)
- Two-part headlines with a punchline reveal (using a period, comma, or "AND" rather than an em dash)
- Topics that have cross-demographic relevance (higher non-follower reach potential)

### Step 3: Caption Writing
For each selected headline, write a full caption following the 8-part structure. Each caption must:
- Stand alone as valuable content without the brand mention
- Include a verified scientific or survey source (placed after the closing line, before the hashtags)
- Integrate the brand in the final third using the appropriate model (A, B, or C)
- End with 2-3 thematic hashtags
- Contain no emojis and no em dashes anywhere in the caption

### Step 4: Quality Check
Before delivering, verify each caption passes:
- [ ] Worth reading without the brand mention?
- [ ] Science claim specific and credible enough?
- [ ] Brand pivot feels like a logical conclusion, not an ad?
- [ ] Tone empowering/validating. Not fear-based or shame-driven?
- [ ] Closing line emotionally resonant?
- [ ] Any regulated health claims appropriately softened?
- [ ] Zero emojis in the caption (including the source line)?
- [ ] Zero em dashes (—) anywhere. Hyphens "-" only for bullets and compound words?
- [ ] Bullets use the plain "-" character, not "•" or other glyphs?
- [ ] Brand integration is in position 5 (before closing), source is in position 7 (after closing, before hashtags)?
- [ ] Only 2-3 hashtags total?

## Hashtag Patterns

- Use 2-3 hashtags total. No more.
- Mix: 1 topic/condition tag + 1 emotional/cultural or trend tag, optionally plus the brand handle as a third tag
- Keep thematic, not generic (#WomensHealth vs #Health, #HormoneOptimization vs #Wellness)
- Cultural hashtags that work well: #GLP1Girlies, #HormonePlotTwist, #AccidentalGlowUp, #NotLazy, #LooksmaxxingEra

## Image Prompt Considerations

When Stu also requests image concepts for a post, the brand's own visual identity always comes first. The default aesthetic below is only the fallback when no brand-specific direction exists.

**Step 1: Pull brand-specific visual direction**
Before drafting any image prompt, check whether the brand has a dedicated context skill installed (e.g., `lotus-brand-context`, `enhanced-brand-context`, `lifemd-brand-context`, `physicians-choice-brand-context`, `protocole-brand-context`, `atkins-brand-context`). If one exists, read it and pull:
- The brand's photography style and tone (cinematic, editorial, candid, clinical, etc.)
- Color palette and treatment (mono, warm-neutral, saturated, pastel, etc.)
- Lighting and composition references
- Subject conventions (product-forward, lifestyle, abstract, talent-led)
- Any explicit dos and don'ts the brand has documented

The image prompt should reflect THAT brand's visual world, not a generic category template.

**Step 2: If no brand context is available**
Ask Stu for visual references or check the brand's website and Instagram before drafting. Do not invent a look.

**Step 3: Default aesthetic for @girls partner posts (fallback only)**
Use this only when the post is a generic @girls fact post with no brand partner attached, or when the brand has no documented visual identity:
- Lifestyle photography. Relatable, candid, warm
- Clean editorial flat-lays for product-adjacent content
- Diverse women in real-life situations (not stock photo energy)

**Image prompt structure for Manus or similar tools:**
> [Style descriptor] + [Subject and composition] + [Lighting] + [Color/tone] + [Lens/format] + [Constraints: no text, no logos, format ratio]
